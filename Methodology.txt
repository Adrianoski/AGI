Methodology

We propose a Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipeline organised around a two-level
semantic index. The lower level is the ordinary embedding of document chunks into
a dense vector space; the upper level is a coarser partition of those chunks into
clusters that we call Semantic Language Maps (SLMs). The motivation is that, in a
standard RAG system, every query is scored against every chunk in the corpus, and
both the latency and the signal-to-noise ratio of the top-k degrade as the corpus
grows. Our hypothesis is that, when a corpus has intrinsic semantic structure
(chapters, topics, sub-domains), a first stage of semantic routing can isolate a
small, homogeneous neighbourhood of chunks before any chunk-level retrieval takes
place, reducing the search space by an order of magnitude while preserving, or
improving, the quality of the retrieved evidence. The variant on which we focus
in this paper, SLM-Full, takes this idea to its extreme: once the relevant SLMs
are identified, the entire union of their chunks is passed to the language model
without any further intra-pool ranking.

An SLM is a cluster of semantically related chunks accompanied by two distinct
vector representations. The first, the centroid embedding, is the normalised mean
of the embeddings of the chunks belonging to the cluster, and is used for cluster
maintenance: deciding whether a new chunk should join an existing cluster, and
deciding when two clusters have drifted close enough to be merged. The second,
the summary embedding, is the embedding of a short list of keywords automatically
extracted from the cluster's content, and is used exclusively for query routing.
This separation is deliberate: averaging dense embeddings tends to wash out the
specific topical signal of a cluster, whereas the keyword string concentrates the
discriminative lexical material that a user query is most likely to match.

The ingestion phase begins with a PDF parsed into Markdown so that header
information is preserved. A lightweight heuristic classifies the document as a
paper, book, technical manual, or generic text on the basis of weighted lexical
signals over the first eight thousand characters; this classification selects an
adaptive chunking profile, which fixes the chunk size and overlap. A regular
recursive splitter then partitions the text along Markdown headers, paragraphs,
and sentences, and a chapter detector annotates each chunk with the title of the
section it belongs to. The chunks are encoded with the multilingual BAAI/bge-m3
model into 1024-dimensional, L2-normalised vectors and persisted in a ChromaDB
collection together with their metadata.

SLM construction is performed by an automatic dispatcher that selects the most
appropriate strategy on the basis of the chapter metadata. When the document
exposes a clear chapter structure, one SLM is created per chapter and its
centroid is set to the mean embedding of the chapter's chunks. When the document
lacks such structure, the system falls back to an incremental cosine-based
assignment: each chunk is compared to every existing centroid, and either joins
the closest cluster above a similarity threshold or seeds a new cluster; the
centroid is then updated incrementally. A density-based clustering procedure
based on HDBSCAN is also available as a batch alternative for fully unstructured
corpora. After assignment, a merge step iteratively fuses pairs of SLMs whose
centroids exceed a higher similarity threshold, with the resulting centroid
computed as a chunk-count-weighted mean and re-normalised. Once the partition has
stabilised, each SLM is endowed with a topical summary: an extractor based on
spaCy's Italian model traverses the cluster, retains named entities of selected
types and content lemmas (proper and common nouns) not already covered by
entities, deduplicates them with substring absorption, and applies a light
quality filter. The resulting keyword list is concatenated into a single string
and embedded to produce the summary embedding stored in the SLM registry.

At query time, the user question is encoded with the same embedding model, and
the top-N SLMs are retrieved by cosine similarity against the summary embeddings
of the registry. In the SLM-Full configuration, the union of the chunks of these
SLMs is then passed in its entirety to the language model: no further dense
re-ranking, BM25 scoring, or fusion is applied within the pool. The intuition is
that the routing stage has already enforced topical coherence, so providing the
generator with the full local context maximises the chance that all the
ground-truth evidence reaches the prompt window, at the cost of a longer context.
Generation is performed by a Qwen-3.5 4B model under a system prompt that forces
English output, so that downstream evaluation does not suffer from a language
mismatch with the English ground-truth answers.

The pipeline is evaluated on a benchmark of 24 questions over an history
textbook, with each question paired to an English ground truth and to a list of
expected keywords. Two families of metrics are reported. Efficiency is measured
through the average retrieval time, the average generation time, and the size of
the chunk pool actually examined. SLM-Full reduces the retrieval pool from 219
chunks to 12 chunks on average and brings retrieval latency down from 20.6 ms to
1.8 ms, an 11.4x speed-up; generation latency increases moderately, from 3801 ms
to 4489 ms, because the model now ingests a richer local context. Lexical
coverage of the expected keywords is 64%, slightly above the 62% of the standard
baseline. Quality is measured with the RAGAS framework using GPT-4o as a judge,
restricted to the two metrics for which our evaluation produced reliable
per-query results: Contextual Precision, which quantifies how much of the
retrieved context is genuinely relevant, and Faithfulness, which quantifies the
extent to which the generated answer is grounded in the retrieved context.
SLM-Full attains Faithfulness scores comparable to the standard baseline on the
queries where both modes return an answer, while its Contextual Precision is
slightly lower on average, reflecting the deliberate trade-off of forwarding the
entire SLM neighbourhood rather than a filtered top-k.
